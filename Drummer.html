The Drummer

Questions to ask your future drummer

Below are a list of questions that you should ask your potential drummer. These questions are designed to get a feel for their experience, dedication, and personality. I cannot tell you what the right answers are or how you should interpret their answers, but I can tell you that these are the questions that have helped other bands determine that I was not the right fit for them.

  • How long have you been playing the drums?
  • Have you ever performed live? or What is your band history?
  • What are your long-term goals as a drummer?
  • What is your personal life like? (This is to determine if they have a hectic life that could bleed into the band)
  • What styles of music are you most comfortable playing?
  • What are your favorite bands?
  • Are you open to collaborating on songwriting and arrangements?
  • Do you usually write down your drum parts or go by memory?
  • How many times a week do you typically practice?
  • What is your usual availability during the week?
  • Do you have your own drum kit? If yes, ask "Would you be more comfortable if we practiced at your place or a practice studio?" (If yes to that one, ask if they have room for the rest of the band) If no, ask "Where do you usually practice?"
  • How well do you handle constructive criticism?
  • What would your ideal band be?
  • If politics could disrupt the band, ask "What kind of benefit shows they feel comfortable playing?" to subtly find out their political views.

Activities

Below are a list of activities to do that could help. However, the only ones that I would consider as required are the ones marked as musical. The other activities are fun and good for bonding, but not guaranteed to help. Adjust any of these activities as you see fit because these are just the outlines of what I would do.

Activity Name Description musical or bonding?
Solo Play This activity is the most important and probably the most obvious. Have them play something for you. It could be an entire song, a solo, or just a groove. Also, let them know to let loose and imagine they are on stage so you can get an idea for their stage presence. musical
Attend a Concert Together This activity is a great way to bond over similar tastes in music. It also gives you a chance to see how they behave in a social setting. Do they seem respectful to others? If you go to a rock or metal concert, do they mosh? If they do, do they follow the mosh etiquette of picking someone up when they fall? This is a good way to tell if they are a good person. bonding
Group Practice This is another crucial activity. You need to play with them to see how well they fit in with the rest of the band. Do they listen to the other musicians, interpret the feel of the song, and adjust their playing accordingly? Do they play too loud or too soft? For Drummers, this is especially important because they are the backbone. Do they keep time well? Is their timing consistent? musical
Social Hangout (e.g., coffee, dinner, karaoke) This activity is good for getting to know the person outside of a musical setting. It helps to see if you have similar interests and if you can get along on a personal level. Remember, you will be spending a lot of time with this person if they join the band, so it's important to make sure you like them as a person. The activities listed are just suggestions. Pick something that you will all enjoy. Although, karaoke is a great way to find out if they can loosen up, and if they have stage fright that they aren't telling you about. bonding
Improvization Have them improvise a solo or groove. This will show you how well they can think on their feet and how creative they are. Trust me, you don't want to be stuck with a Ringo. You want someone who can actually help with the songwriting process. musical
Team-building Activities (e.g., escape room, pictionary/charades) These activities are great for bonding and learning how to work together as a team. If they can work with others and if they will work with your bandmates are two different things. Everyone communicates differently, and these activities can help the new person learn how to communicate with the others. Plus, these activities are fun and can help break the ice. bonding
